## 1. Overview

Humidity resistance at 95% relative humidity (RH) represents one of the most demanding environmental tests for cryptocurrency hardware wallets and backup devices. This standard establishes requirements for electronic devices and materials to maintain functionality and structural integrity when exposed to extreme moisture conditions that can cause corrosion, electrical failures, and material degradation.

At 95% RH, the air contains moisture levels approaching saturation, creating conditions where condensation can form on surfaces even with minimal temperature fluctuations. For hardware wallets containing sensitive electronics, cryptographic processors, and user interfaces, such conditions present multiple failure mechanisms including electrochemical corrosion, dendrite growth between circuit traces, swelling of polymer materials, delamination of bonded interfaces, and degradation of electrical contacts.

The importance of humidity resistance in cryptocurrency security devices extends beyond simple functionality. A hardware wallet that fails due to humidity exposure may become unrecoverable, potentially resulting in permanent loss of stored cryptocurrency assets. Unlike conventional electronics where data can often be recovered from damaged devices, the cryptographic security model of hardware wallets means that physical device failure can equate to total asset loss if proper backup procedures were not followed.

This SafeScoring criterion ensures that evaluated products can survive and operate reliably in humid environments worldwide, from tropical climates to seasonal humidity spikes in temperate regions. The standard draws from IEC 60068-2-78 for steady-state humid heat testing and incorporates relevant specifications for electronics environmental qualification.

### Environmental Context and Global Relevance

Humidity conditions vary dramatically across global regions where cryptocurrency users operate:

**Tropical Climates (Southeast Asia, Central America, Central Africa):**
- Average RH: 70-90%
- Peak RH: 95-100% during monsoon seasons
- Duration: Extended periods of high humidity lasting weeks or months
- Temperature: 25-35C with high humidity creates severe stress conditions

**Coastal Regions (Maritime climates worldwide):**
- Average RH: 60-80%
- Salt-laden humid air accelerates corrosion
- Frequent fog and condensation events
- Marine environment compounds humidity effects

**Temperate Climates with Seasonal Variation:**
- Summer humidity spikes to 80-95% RH
- Indoor humidity can spike during HVAC system failures
- Transition seasons create condensation risks
- Basement and storage area humidity often exceeds ambient

**Desert and Arid Regions:**
- Generally low humidity but extreme diurnal variation
- Occasional humidity spikes during rare precipitation
- Air conditioning creates condensation on cold surfaces
- Imported devices may experience humidity shock

Understanding these environmental contexts informs the testing requirements that ensure hardware wallets function reliably for users regardless of their geographic location or storage conditions.

### Historical Development of Humidity Standards

Environmental testing standards for electronics evolved from military and aerospace requirements in the mid-20th century. The original MIL-STD-810 series established humidity testing procedures that were subsequently adapted for commercial electronics through IEC standards. Key milestones include:

- 1962: MIL-STD-810 first published with humidity test methods
- 1974: IEC 68-2-3 (now IEC 60068-2-78) established international humidity test standards
- 1990s: Consumer electronics industry adopted standardized environmental qualification
- 2010s: Cryptocurrency hardware manufacturers began formal environmental testing programs
- 2020s: SafeScoring integration of humidity requirements into security device evaluation

### Detailed Test Methodology

**Pre-Test Conditioning:**
1. Visual inspection and documentation of initial condition
2. Baseline electrical measurements (insulation resistance, contact resistance)
3. Functional verification - all features operating correctly
4. Weight measurement of device and critical components
5. Stabilization at 23C +/- 2C, 50% +/- 5% RH for minimum 24 hours

**Test Chamber Requirements:**
- Chamber volume: Minimum 10x device volume
- Humidity generation: Steam injection or ultrasonic atomization
- Humidity control: +2/-3% RH accuracy with PID control
- Temperature uniformity: +/- 1C throughout working volume
- Air velocity: 0.5-1.0 m/s to ensure uniform conditions
- Monitoring: Continuous recording at minimum 1-minute intervals

**Test Procedure (IEC 60068-2-78):**
1. Place specimens in chamber at standard conditions
2. Raise temperature to 40C at max 1C/minute
3. Simultaneously raise humidity to 95% RH at max 5%/minute
4. Maintain steady-state conditions for specified duration
5. Perform in-situ functional tests at 24-hour intervals
6. Monitor for condensation and adjust ramp rates if needed
7. At test completion, ramp down to standard conditions
8. Allow 1-2 hour recovery before final evaluation

**In-Situ Testing During Exposure:**
- Power-on verification every 24 hours
- Display readability check
- Button/touch interface functionality
- Communication interface verification (USB, Bluetooth)
- Cryptographic operation verification (sign test transaction)

**Post-Test Evaluation:**
- Visual inspection at 10x magnification
- Electrical measurements comparison to baseline
- Full functional test suite
- Disassembly inspection (for qualification units only)
- Accelerated corrosion check (for extended qualification)

### Material and Design Requirements

**Printed Circuit Board (PCB):**
- Conformal coating: Acrylic, silicone, or urethane minimum 25 micron thickness
- Surface finish: ENIG (Electroless Nickel Immersion Gold) or OSP with conformal coat
- Laminate: FR-4 or higher grade with low moisture absorption
- Solder mask: Liquid photoimageable (LPI) with humidity resistance rating

**Enclosure Design:**
- IP rating: Minimum IP54, recommended IP65 for full compliance
- Gasket materials: Silicone or EPDM with compression set resistance
- Seal design: Continuous perimeter seal preferred over point sealing
- Vent design: Gore-Tex or equivalent membrane if venting required
- Cable entries: Strain relief with moisture seal

**Component Selection:**
- Connectors: Gold or nickel-plated contacts with IP-rated housings
- Switches: Sealed membrane or IP-rated mechanical
- Displays: Sealed module with moisture-resistant adhesive
- Batteries: Hermetically sealed cells, avoid vented designs
- Sensors: Conformal-coated or inherently sealed designs

**Corrosion Prevention:**
- Dissimilar metals separated by barrier or conformal coat
- Galvanic series consideration in material selection
- Flux residue removal to prevent ionic contamination
- Humidity indicators inside enclosure for field verification

**Desktop Computers:**

### Backup Solutions

**Stainless Steel Plates (Cryptosteel, Billfodl):**
Marine-grade stainless steel (316) provides excellent humidity resistance. The passive metal surface resists corrosion even at 95% RH. No electronic components means no electrical failure modes. Extended humidity exposure may cause superficial surface effects but not functional degradation. These products are rated for underwater storage (beyond humidity requirements). Full compliance with humidity requirements is inherent to material selection.

**Titanium Plates:**
Titanium forms a stable oxide layer providing exceptional humidity and corrosion resistance. No degradation expected even in extreme humidity or salt-laden atmospheres. These products exceed any humidity requirement by significant margins.

**Paper and Cardstock:**
Paper materials absorb moisture and can become illegible at high humidity. Ink may run or transfer. Paper strength decreases significantly when wet. Paper-based backups should never be stored in humid conditions. Lamination provides some protection but seams remain vulnerable. Fundamentally unsuitable for humid environments.
